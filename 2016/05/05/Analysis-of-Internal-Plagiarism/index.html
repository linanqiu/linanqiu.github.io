Analysis of Internal Plagiarism

This is an attempt to quantify the situation of internal plagiarism in a class I TA. Internal plagiarism happens when a student taking the class submits a homework that is highly similar to that of another student.

This similarity is measured using JPlag that constructs an abstract syntax tree out of the Java source code, considers the base code that instructors provides, and provides a measure of similarity (between 0% and 100%) of all students with homework submissions. I then use my own processing tool to organize this into a dataset, excluding any similarity pairs that are below 80% to ensure that most if not all cases considered are unambiguous.

There are 6 homeworks published this semester, with a total class enrollment of 330 students (281 in section 1, 49 in section 2) by the end of the semester.

Number of Cheating Students Over Homeworks

First, I am interested in the number of students who are identified as cheaters in each of the homeworks.

In Entire Course

Unfortunately, the number of cheaters did not decrease across homeworks. Instead, the number increased. This is plain disappointing.

Across Sections

I decompose this trend across the two sections.

It is evident that the number of cheaters in section 1 increased while hte number in section 2 remained steady.

To obtain a better picture, I can look at the percentage of students who cheated in both sections.

[281, 49]

Forst most homeworks, section 1 has a far higher proportion of students cheating than section 2.

Repeat Offenders

I also want to find out the number of repeat offenders (i.e. students who have cheated on multiple homeworks). This is an important indicator because a student cheating across 4 or more homeworks (more than half a semester) would only do so if an instructor did not approach the student at all throughout the semester or he is simply brazen / courageous beyond my comprehension.

In Entire Course

We see that there are significant number of students who cheated on 4 or more homeworks. This means that for half of the semester or more, they consistently produced plagiarized homework.

Across Sections

Again, I decompose this trend across the two sections.

I find that consistent cheating is much more prevalent in section 1 than 2 by both absolute figures and percentage terms. In fact, section 2 had no students cheating on 4 or more homeworks. All the severe offenders came from section 1.
